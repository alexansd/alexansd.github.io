Cyberbullying:
The Always-On Threat

Unlike playground bullying, online harassment follows teens into their homes, their bedrooms, and every hour of the day.

What Makes It Different

Traditional bullying ends when school ends. Cyberbullying does not. Harassment can reach a teen at any hour through any device. Posts, screenshots, and group chat messages can spread instantly to hundreds of peers, making humiliation feel total and inescapable.

The anonymity or perceived distance of online interactions can also cause people to say things they would never say face to face — lowering the threshold for cruelty while raising the perceived risk for the target.

📌   Approximately 1 in 5 teenagers reports being cyberbullied. Less than half tell a parent or trusted adult. Silence is the norm — which is why your teen needs to know the door is always open.

Forms of Cyberbullying

  • Harassment — Repeated sending of offensive, threatening, or hurtful messages
  • Exclusion — Deliberately leaving someone out of group chats or tagging everyone except them
  • Outing — Sharing someone's private information or images without consent
  • Impersonation — Creating fake profiles or sending messages pretending to be someone else
  • Cyberstalking — Persistent monitoring, tracking, or threatening online behavior
  • Pile-ons — Coordinated mass criticism or mocking of a post or comment

Warning Signs in Your Teen

Many teens won't volunteer that they're being bullied online — out of shame, fear that devices will be taken away, or believing they should handle it themselves. Watch for:

  • Visible emotional distress during or after using their phone
  • Suddenly stopping use of apps they normally loved
  • Unusual secretiveness about online activity
  • Social withdrawal, especially from friends they know online
  • Declining academic performance without explanation
  • Changes in mood, sleep, or appetite

How to Respond If It's Happening

Don't react with panic

Your first response sets the tone. Express that you're glad they told you. Stay calm so they feel safe sharing the full picture.

Document everything

Screenshot messages and posts before reporting or blocking. Evidence is important if the situation escalates to school administrators or police.

Report and block

Every major platform has reporting tools. Use them. Blocking the bully prevents further direct contact and often disrupts the behavior.

Involve the school

Even if the bullying happens off-campus, schools have obligations to address behavior that affects the school environment. Document and report.

Most importantly, make it clear to your teenager — repeatedly and sincerely — that being bullied is never their fault, and that coming to you will never result in punishment.
