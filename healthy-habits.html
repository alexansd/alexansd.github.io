A Tool, Not
a Villain

Social media usage can be healthy once boundaries and limitations are set, and parents know how to discuss healthy usage with their kids. The goal is confident, intentional use — not fear.

How Social Media Can Be Used Productively

While social media can be addictive for kids and teens, it doesn't have to be seen as the enemy. When used intentionally, it offers real value for adolescent development (University of Rochester Medicine, 2025):

  • Finding community: Adolescence is a time of self-discovery — exploring identity, interests, and hobbies. Social media allows teens to connect with others who share their interests in ways that are often impossible outside the online world. For marginalized groups, these shared spaces can offer a sense of belonging and affirmation of racial, sexual, or gender identity.
  • Reaching out to friends and family: For teens with social anxiety, building friendships online may feel more accessible than in person. While online connection should not replace real-world social skills, it can still be a meaningful and useful supplement.
  • Creativity and self-expression: Social media can serve as a creative platform where teens share art, writing, or other talents. Positive encouragement and feedback can build confidence in young people who want their voices heard.
  • Spreading messages and advocacy: Social media can be used to engage with causes that matter to the individual. That sense of community and social proactivity is especially important as teens begin to explore their deeper beliefs and values.

Healthy Habits for Social Media Use

Focus on real-life friends

Social media is great for connecting with online communities, but it's important to balance that time with in-person relationships. Communicating online and in-person are not the same, and nurturing real-life relationships is essential for developing social skills (Harris, 2022).

Be intentional and aware

It's easy to fall into patterns of "doomscrolling." Teens can avoid this by being purposeful about their time on social media and mindful about what content they are actively choosing to engage with (Harris, 2022).

Teach social media literacy

Teaching social media literacy allows teens to develop critical thinking skills — questioning the intention behind a post, or whether what they are looking at is realistic (Harris, 2022).

Model healthy social media use

Children take their social media habits from their parents. In order for kids to have healthy habits online, parents need to set the example by practicing healthy habits themselves (Harris, 2022).
