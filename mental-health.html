Social Media &
Teen Mental Health

Social media offers opportunities for connection and belonging — but it also carries serious risks to adolescent self-esteem, body image, and psychological wellbeing.

Social Support vs. Social Comparison

While social media can produce opportunities for social support and a sense of belonging, it can also be a source of social comparison, cyberbullying, and the fear of missing out (FOMO). These forces have measurable impacts on adolescents' self-esteem, body image, and overall psychological wellbeing. The negative aspects of social media can lead to increased rates of loneliness, anxiety, and depression — especially for younger individuals who are not yet well-versed in media literacy (Qadrie et al., 2025).

Adolescents are especially vulnerable to these effects because they are actively working to develop their identities and are more susceptible to peer influences than any other age group.

Social Comparison & Body Image

Social media users are constantly exposed to perfectly curated images and videos of other people's lives, which can induce feelings of inferiority and inadequacy. Photo-editing tools and filters compound this problem: they increase body dissatisfaction in adolescents, leading to body shame, concerns about weight, and disordered eating. Online harassment further damages adolescents' self-esteem (Qadrie et al., 2025).

📌   Research has found that, especially for young girls, excessive social media use significantly increases the risk of depression. There is a clear relationship between the amount of time spent online and increased depressive symptoms (Ciobanu et al., 2025).

FOMO: Fear of Missing Out

Social media is designed to share events and experiences — but constantly viewing peers' updates can make adolescents feel excluded and lonely. This "fear of missing out" is not incidental; it is a structural feature of how these platforms present information, and it disproportionately affects teens who are already socially anxious or seeking peer validation (Qadrie et al., 2025).

Sleep, Attention & Broader Harms

Excessive social media use has been linked to disordered eating, sleep disruption, attention problems, and symptoms of depression, anxiety, and loneliness. Poor sleep during adolescence is correlated with emotional health issues and a higher risk of suicide. Parents can create household rules that limit phone use after a certain hour at night to protect their teen's sleep quality (Katella, 2024).

What Parents Can Do

Teach critical media evaluation

Help your kids critically evaluate the content they consume. Introduce them to online spaces that foster positive and inclusive environments (Qadrie et al., 2025).

Set screen time limits

Parents can reduce depression risk by imposing limits that prevent children from spending excessive time on social media (Ciobanu et al., 2025).

Have open discussions

Talk with your children about positive ways to interact on social media, how to report cyberbullying, not sharing personal information online, and how to question what they view (Katella, 2024).

Model healthy use

Parents need to be role models in displaying healthy social media behavior. Follow the same rules that you set for your child (Katella, 2024).
