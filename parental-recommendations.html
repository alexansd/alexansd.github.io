What Parents
Can Do

Research-backed strategies for guiding your teen's social media use — from spotting early warning signs to building lasting confidence in both your child and yourself.

Watch for Signs of Problematic Use

Determining problematic social media use in its early stages can help prevent larger issues from developing. Warning signs include neglected responsibilities, chronic sleep deprivation, and reduced face-to-face interaction. More behavioral indicators include an inability to stop using social media even when the teen intends to, strong cravings to be online, and deceptive behavior in order to gain access to devices or platforms.

If any of these signs are present, or if your child reports feeling this way themselves, it may be time to re-evaluate their relationship with social media. If the level of distress feels serious, consider contacting a mental health professional to review treatment strategies (American Psychological Association, 2023).

📌   When children understand the consequences associated with a rule, they are more likely to follow it. If they understand why you are placing boundaries on social media use, they are more likely to respect those boundaries (Dunham et al., 2020).

Set Boundaries — Together

Sit down with your children to discuss your expectations around social media use and the reasoning behind them. Consider what feels appropriate: Do you want to be more restrictive, or do you simply want your child to be cautious and informed? Whatever the answer, talk it through with them openly. Discuss the consequences associated with problematic use, and offer your child ideas for productive alternatives to social media that keep them engaged and occupied (Dunham et al., 2020).

When children understand the "why" behind a rule, compliance increases significantly. Collaboration builds trust, and trust makes those rules more durable over time.

Model Appropriate Use

Children learn their social media habits from their parents — for better or for worse. If a parent scrolls social media for hours while neglecting responsibilities, a child will absorb that as the norm. If you believe your child should only be on social media for a set amount of time each day, hold yourself to the same standard. If you expect them to finish their daily responsibilities before turning on a screen, make sure your own tasks are completed first. The parent is where a child learns the most (American Psychological Association, 2023).

Help Your Children Find Their Confidence

Research links social media addiction with appearance anxiety through the mechanism of self-rumination — prolonged, unwanted thinking about negatives or stressors. Social media algorithms compound this problem by continuously surfacing similar content, making it easy for a teen already focused on a perceived flaw to encounter a steady stream of reinforcing material. To counter this, work with your children to identify and articulate their genuine strengths. Ask them to journal what they are good at. Make sure they are aware of how social media can distort self-image so they can actively protect themselves (Wang et al., 2026).

Protect Your Own Confidence Too

It is also worth considering your own relationship with social media. Just as comparison harms teens, it can harm parents. Social comparison is not inherently negative — research distinguishes between two types of envy it can produce. Benign envy drives you toward a goal you see in someone else and supports self-improvement. Malicious envy leaves you stuck, resentful, and focused on what you lack rather than what you have (Lee et al., 2024).

When you encounter content online that makes you feel inadequate, try to use it as motivation rather than a measure of your worth. Recognize that the most idealized outcomes seen on social media are often impossible or unrealistic. Modeling this mindset for your children — openly, in conversation — is one of the most effective lessons you can teach them about using social media well (Lee et al., 2024).

Five Core Recommendations at a Glance

Catch problems early

Monitor for neglected responsibilities, sleep disruption, cravings, and deceptive behavior around device use. Early intervention is far more effective than late (American Psychological Association, 2023).

Explain the "why"

Children who understand the reasoning behind rules are significantly more likely to follow them. Talk through consequences, not just commands (Dunham et al., 2020).

Hold yourself to the same standard

Your own screen habits set the baseline. If you want your child to put the phone down at dinner, you must do the same (American Psychological Association, 2023).

Build identity outside social media

Help your teen find sources of confidence — sports, arts, volunteering — that are not dependent on likes or follower counts. These are documented buffers against social media harm (Wang et al., 2026).

Reframe comparison as inspiration

Teach your child — and practice yourself — that what you see online can be a springboard for growth rather than evidence of inadequacy (Lee et al., 2024).

Know when to seek help

If distress is significant or persistent, a mental health professional who specializes in adolescent development can review strategies and treatment options with your family (American Psychological Association, 2023).
