Privacy, Data &
Online Predators

Teens share far more than they realise — and not just with friends. Understanding digital footprints and grooming tactics is essential for every parent.

The Privacy Problem

Most teenagers have limited awareness of how much data they generate and who collects it. Every like, search, location check-in, and profile detail contributes to a detailed behavioral profile used for highly targeted advertising — and potentially sold to third parties.

Beyond commercial data collection, publicly shared personal details — school name, neighborhood, sports team, daily routine — can be pieced together by anyone with the intent to locate or contact a young person.

📌   A study of teen social media profiles found that 82% included enough information for a stranger to identify their school, neighborhood, or daily schedule.

Understanding Grooming

Online grooming is the process by which an adult builds trust and emotional connection with a child over time, with the intent to exploit them. It is deliberate, patient, and often undetectable to the teen — and sometimes even to parents monitoring activity.

Groomers use social platforms, gaming communities, and messaging apps. They study their targets' public profiles to personalise their approach, often presenting as a peer or slightly older teen.

Grooming Tactics to Know

  • Flattery and attention — Excessive compliments, making the teen feel uniquely seen and understood
  • Secrets and isolation — Encouraging the teen to keep the relationship private from parents
  • Gift-giving — Gaming credits, gift cards, or other incentives that create a sense of obligation
  • Gradual boundary-testing — Slowly introducing sexual topics or requesting images to normalise the behavior
  • Exploiting vulnerability — Targeting teens who seem lonely, troubled, or seeking validation

Platform Privacy Basics

Set accounts to private

Review privacy settings on every platform your teen uses. Public accounts expose posts, followers, and contact options to strangers.

Turn off location sharing

Many apps request location access. Check both the app settings and your phone's location permissions for each social app.

Audit profile information

Look at your teen's public profile as a stranger would. Remove school names, sports clubs, neighborhoods, and schedules.

Talk about unknown contacts

Establish a norm: any adult who reaches out to your teen online should be told to you. No exceptions, no judgment.

If You Suspect Something

If your teen is receiving messages from unknown adults, becoming secretive about a specific online "friend," receiving unexplained gifts, or showing signs of emotional manipulation, take it seriously immediately. Contact the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (NCMEC) at 1-800-THE-LOST or report to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) at ic3.gov. Do not delete evidence before reporting.
