Privacy, Data &
Online Predators

Social media platforms promise safety — but who reads the terms and conditions? Understanding the real risks of online predators and privacy exposure is essential for every parent.

Safety Is Not Guaranteed

Social media platforms like TikTok and Instagram require users to accept terms and conditions that include the platform's safety policy — but very few parents or children actually read them. The reality is that safety is not guaranteed simply because a parent has permitted their child to use a platform. Beyond the platforms' own data practices, parents must also be vigilant about dangerous people online (Marcum, 2007).

Child predators are people who use deceitful and manipulative tactics to reach their victims. While predators still operate in the physical world, social media has made it significantly easier for them to seek out and make contact with children (Tynes, 2007).

How Predators Communicate: A Real Example

It can be very difficult for parents to spot the warning signs of online predators. They often present themselves as young peers who seem relatable and trustworthy. In reality, their behavior follows a calculated pattern of manipulation. Research by Marcum (2007) outlines case studies of communications between predators and actors posing as children. The following exchange illustrates how a predator works to establish trust while pushing boundaries:

courtneyd132004: cn we talk on the fone first? flamningdonkeybutt: sure tomorrow k courtneyd132004: wats ur # flamningdonkeybutt: [number provided] flamningdonkeybutt: good night baby courtneyd132004: im afraid 2 give it out flamningdonkeybutt: dont be afraid flamningdonkeybutt: I wont do anything u dont want me to do

Source: Marcum (2007). The "courtneyd132004" account was operated by a researcher posing as a child.

This exchange demonstrates several key predatory tactics in action: the push for personal contact information, affectionate language designed to create emotional attachment, and direct reassurance aimed at neutralizing the child's instinct to be cautious.

Warning Signs to Watch For

Based on research into predatory behavior online, parents should be alert to the following warning signs (Marcum, 2007):

  • Online friends or contacts who ask to exchange personal information and are not known to the parent
  • Anyone attempting to solicit images from your child by any means
  • Adults showing an unusual or intense level of care and interest in a child's comfort — a pattern consistent with grooming designed to make the child feel safe with them
  • Contacts who introduce inappropriate subjects, such as virginity, being alone together, or sending photos
  • Anyone older than 18 who attempts to maintain ongoing contact with your child in a way that is kept private from parents

📌   Risk factors that make children more susceptible to online abuse include broken or unstable family situations, parents with limited awareness of digital risks, and families who do not adequately monitor their children's screen time and privacy settings (Aksoy et al., 2025).

Why Children Seek Out Social Media

Understanding what draws adolescents to social media is essential context for parents. Children most commonly turn to social media for the following reasons (Alexander, 2015):

  • A lack of parental supervision that leaves unstructured time online
  • A need for connection, friendships, and relationships
  • A desire to feel understood and accepted by others

Predators are aware of these motivations and deliberately exploit them — presenting themselves as uniquely understanding, attentive, and non-judgmental in order to fill the emotional gap a child is seeking to fill.

Parental Mediation of Social Media

Research into the associations between parental mediation strategies and children's social media outcomes reveals that it is not enough for a parent to simply talk with their child about online risks. The following patterns were identified when examining what actually produces better outcomes (Corcoran et al., 2022):

  • Education and consistency: While educating children about online risks is important, the consistency with which a parent implements and reinforces those conversations is equally critical. Irregular or one-off discussions are significantly less effective.
  • Merely talking shows limited impact: Conversations about social media risks alone — without accompanying rules or structure — show less significant protective impact on children's actual behavior.
  • Establishing house rules: Setting clear, consistent household rules around social media use correlates with meaningfully better outcomes for children.
  • Co-use: Parents who actively use social media alongside their children — viewing content together, discussing what they see, and demonstrating appropriate use in real time — produce some of the strongest protective effects of any mediation strategy.

Safety Recommendations for Parents

Use filtering and monitoring tools

Website filters and parental control apps can limit which platforms your child can access and allow appropriate monitoring of their online activity (Tynes, 2007).

Communicate openly and consistently

Have ongoing, repeated conversations about the risks of social media — not a single talk. Include what predatory behavior looks like and why certain requests must always be reported to a parent (Tynes, 2007).

Set privacy controls

Limit who can befriend or contact your child online. Enable blocking features and review privacy settings together on every platform your child uses (Tynes, 2007).

Teach the telltale signs

Equip your child to recognize the specific behaviors that signal grooming or predatory intent — as outlined above — so they have the knowledge to act and the confidence to tell you (Tynes, 2007).
